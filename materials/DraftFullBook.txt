Radio Waves
In the Intermediate manual, we learnt that oscillating electrons send out electromagnetic
waves of energy, with oscillations of the electric and magnetic elds. Also, we met the
concept of phase, and phase di erence.
In this Full manual, we explain what is meant by the power ux density of the wave, and
how to calculate it. Also we consider another kind of polarisation, circular polarisation.
Fourier Transforms are in the Full syllabus but we covered them in the Intermediate
Manual. Likewise we discussed sampling of a wave in the Intermediate Manual and saw
the disastrous consequences if the sampling rate is too slow. Here you need to know
about false signals called aliases and how to calculate them.

Electromagnetic Energy
Thanks to the investigations by Michael Faraday, we have seen that an electric current
can create a magnetic eld, while a changing magnetic eld can create a current.
James Clerk Maxwell discovered that there exists a self-restoring, permanent, travelling
disturbance in electric and magnetic elds. Changing electric elds induce changing
magnetic elds induce changing electric elds induce changing magnetic elds and so
on. He calculated that these disturbances travel at the speed of light.
All through the Foundation and Intermediate manuals, we have had the mantra:
oscillating electrons in wires produce electromagnetic waves in space,
electromagnetic waves in space cause electrons in wires to oscillate.
Well, that‚Äôs not strictly true!
Electromagnetic energy propagates in the form of photons. A photon is a bundle or
packet of energy. The energy owing from a source is not continuous but emitted in
packets of energy. We are familiar with light photons but radio too travels in the form of
radio photons. The energy of a photon increases with its frequency, so gamma photons
are the most dangerous and radio photons are the weakest.
https://imagine.gsfc.nasa.gov/science/toolbox/emspectrum2.html
The best example of a radio photon is the emission of radio energy from Jupiter. Radio
astronomers observe the photons of 21 cm wavelength, emitted by hydrogen gas at 1420
MHz, to study the activity of the planet. Another example is in Magnetic Resonance
Imaging, MRI. Researchers at Delft University of Technology have used a
superconducting qubit to listen to the weakest radio signal of all - single radio photons!
(https://www.laserfocusworld.com/detectors-imaging/article/16566810/quantum-circuitdetects-individual-radio-photons)
What is the energy of a radio photon emitted by your handheld radio on 2 metres,
145.500 MHz? Thanks to Max Planck, we can work it out and it comes out to about
1 x 10 to the power of minus 25 joules of energy.
If you are transmitting one watt of power, each second you are sending out 10 septillion
photons, or 10 to the power of positive 25, which is rather a lot. Each photon does not
carry much energy, but if you have enough of them, you de nitely need to consider the
safety aspect.
The wave theory of light could not account for the photoelectric e ect, where electrons
are emitted from a surface when light is shone on it. Einstein explained the phenomenon,

Power Flux Density
This diagram shows a radio wave at an instant in time, with the wavelength as the
distance between two peaks. For example, the wavelength could be 6 m.

Diagram of an electromagnetic wave
If we could measure the electric eld at one particular spot, we would nd the strength of
the eld changes with time in a sinusoidal fashion. If the polarisation is vertical, the
electrical oscillations are up and down. The magnetic eld is in phase with the electric
eld, meaning that they reach maximum strength at the same time, and are both zero at
the same time. E is the vertical electric eld, along the y axis, measured in volts/metre, V/
m. H is the horizontal magnetic eld, along the x axis, measured in A/m. They are not only
at right angles to each other, but also at right angles to the direction of travel, z. The
energy ux, the ow of energy of the electromagnetic wave is in the z direction.
When we multiply the two elds, we obtain the power ux density of the wave, measured
in W/m2, the power per unit area, which is the power falling on one square metre.

Power ux density = E x H
For example, a 3 GHz wave is incident on a surface. What is the power ux density if E is
100 V/m and H is 265 mA/m?
power ux density = 100 x 0.265 = 26.5 W/m2

Impedance of free space

m by H in A/m we get the units V/A, which is ‚Ñ¶. In fact it is the impedance of free space,
equal to 377‚Ñ¶. Check it with the example above - 100 V/m divided by 265 mA/m. Just as
oscillating signals experience impedance when travelling through a coaxial cable, so do
oscillating electromagnetic waves travelling through the vacuum of space. At one time it
was thought that the waves needed a medium to travel through, which they called the
ether.

a solenoid, and the permittivity of free space governs the electric eld between the two

Linear Polarisation

If we are standing along the z axis, we would see the wave travelling towards us or away
from us. The electric eld would be oscillating up and down. If the frequency of the wave
is 1.0 Hz, we would see the electric eld strength oscillate up and down, once every
second. Starting with a maximum value in the up direction, it would decrease to zero after
quarter of a second, change direction, again reaching a maximum value pointing

downwards after half a second, before returning to zero, as shown in the diagram:
If the frequency of the wave is 50 MHz, it would be a blur! We would see a vertical line, as
shown in the diagram. We say the wave is linearly plane polarised, because we just see a
line, in one direction. The plane of oscillation is vertical. The oscillations are in one
direction only.

wave. The diagram shows the electric eld at one eighth of its way in the cycle. It is

How is this possible? Where is the oscillation? The electric eld can have oscillating
components in both the y axis and the x axis. The y component is a maximum when the x

component is zero. A quarter of a cycle later, the y component is now zero and the x
component is a maximum. They are 90 degrees out of phase. At t = 1/8 of the cycle, the
resultant is at 45 degrees because the two components are equal. All the time the two
components are oscillating up and down, side to side, while the resultant electric eld
goes round in a circle. This is the circularly polarised wave.
In the diagram, the wave is coming towards us and the electric eld is rotating in a clockwise

direction. According to the IEEE, the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers, we
say this is left-circular polarisation. If we were to turn round and look at the wave going
away from us, the electric eld would rotate in an anti-clockwise direction, but it is still
left-circularly polarised.
If we are back at the z = 0 position, with the wave travelling away from us, and we see the
electric eld rotating clockwise, then the wave is right-circularly polarised. What would we
see if we turned round and the wave was coming towards us?

Helical Antennas
Helical antennas are used where we cannot be sure of the orientation of the antenna and
hence the polarisation, such as for communicating with spacecraft, satellites and tracking
animals. However the handedness of the receiving antenna must be the same as the
transmitting antenna. A left-polarised antenna must be used for both transmitting and
receiving. If you used a right-hand polarised antenna, the received signal would be weak.
Helical antennas are found on the Moon and Mars. The Insight lander took a sel e at the
end of its mission!

Sampling
Sampling was covered comprehensively in the Intermediate manual, page 22.
According to the Nyquist theorem, a signal is perfectly represented when we sample at a
frequency at least twice the maximum signal frequency.

Sampling frequency > 2 x Maximum signal frequency
In theory, if we sample at a higher rate, there will be no improvement.
What if there are frequencies present which are higher than half of the sampling
frequency. For example, if the signal frequency is 2 kHz we must sample more than 4
kHz. In the Intermediate manual, we gave the example of a sampling frequency of 5 Hz,
so the signal should not have been higher than 2.5 Hz. Unfortunately, a 6 Hz signal was
present! The consequence was a spurious 1 Hz result.
For the Full Licence, you need to understand that this false image is called an alias and
this phenomenon is called aliasing. You also need to be able to calculate it.
There is a nice animation on this wiki page:
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliasing
Activity 1.4 investigating aliasing using GNU Radio
You can demonstrate the e ect of changing the sample rate using GNU Radio, by
showing the graphs in the time and frequency domains.
This website has a brilliant applet which shows where the alias frequency is:
http://webapps.chem.uoa.gr/efs/applets/AppletNyquist/Appl_Nyquist2.html
The frequency spectrum in the applet shows the signal frequency, the sampling frequency
and Nyquist frequency, which is half the sampling frequency. For example, change the
signal frequency to 10.0 Hz. Now change the sampling frequency to 20.0 Hz. What has
happened? It has sampled at all the zero points! So no sample is produced.
It is like a stroboscopic e ect. When you see the spokes of a wheel on a lm, they seem
to be stationary, if the shutter rate on the camera matches the rotation of the spokes. The
sampling of an analog to digital converter ADC works in the same way, taking samples of
a changing electrical signal.

Where is the alias frequency?
The wiki page, the applet and GNU Radio can give you a good idea of how aliases are
produced. To calculate the alias frequency, it is quite simple.
fo = signal frequency
fs = sampling frequency
N = Nyquist frequency = 1/2 of the sampling frequency = fs/2
fA = alias frequency

fA = N - (fo - N) = 2N - fo = fs - fo
Copyright ¬© October 2025 John Hislop

19
For example, the sampling frequency = 10 kHz. The signal frequency fo= 5.1 kHz
N = 5.0 kHz
fA = 5.0 - (5.1 - 5.0) = 5.0 - 0.1 = 4.9 kHz
or
fA = fs - fo = 10.0 - 5.1 = 4.9 kHz
There will be an alias frequency at 4.9 kHz. From a symmetry point of view, the signal
frequency is 0.1 Hz above the Nyquist frequency, so the alias is 0.1 Hz below the Nyquist
frequency.
Where does the formula come from? The simple answer is symmetry. The alias frequency
is as much below N as the signal frequency is above. If there was a di erence of 200 Hz
above, then the alias would be 200 Hz below. We saw that graphically on the frequency
spectrum.
Where does the formula come from, really? The explanation is beyond the speci cation. If
you want to know, go to this web page. You need to copy and paste it. http://
www.ee.ic.ac.uk/pcheung/teaching/DE2_EE/Lecture 5 - Sampling (notes).pdf
What happens if the sampling frequency is less than the signal frequency?
We tried signal frequency = 20.0 Hz, sampling frequency = 14.0 Hz.
There was an alias at 6.0 Hz. Does the formula still work? The symmetry appears to be
broken.
fA = 2N - fo = 14.0 - 20.0 = -6.0 Hz.
We get a negative answer, so we must take the magnitude, which is 6.0 Hz.
Try another one. fo = 10.0 Hz, fs = 8.0 Hz.
fA = 8.0 - 10.0 = - 2.0 Hz.
So the alias is at 2.0 Hz, which we can see nicely on the frequency spectrum.

Anti-alias frequency lters
What is the highest frequency present in your signal? If you think you will experience alias
frequencies distorting your signal, you need to know the range of frequencies in your
signal. For telephony, ordinary voice signals will be limited to 3 kHz, so you would sample
at higher than 6 kHz, ideally 2.5 to 3 times higher. You would use a low pass lter to make
sure that there are no frequencies higher than 3 kHz in your signal. Such a lter is called
an anti-alias lter, used to eliminate or suppress any aliases.
If there are frequencies in your signal higher than the Nyquist frequency, half the sampling
rate, then they will not be sampled and they will create alias frequencies, distorting your
sampled signal. Once you know your upper limit of frequency, you can design an
appropriate lter.

Radio Waves Answers
1. An electromagnetic wave is travelling towards you. It is a right handed circularly
polarised wave. This means that the electric eld is rotating in an anti-clockwise
directions.
2. We obtain the power density of an electromagnetic wave by multiplying the electric
eld strength in V/m by the magnetic eld strength is A/m.
3. An electromagnetic wave has a power density of 100 W/m2. If the electric eld strength
Is 5 V/m, the magnetic eld strength is 100/5, which is 20 A/m.
4. The phase relationship between the electric and magnetic elds of an electromagnetic
wave is that they are in phase, both reaching a maximum at the same time.
5. An audio signal contains frequencies up to 3 kHz. Therefore the recommended Nyquist
rate for sampling is twice that, 6 Hz.
6. If you sample at a lower frequency than the Nyquist rate, you are liable to get false
images or aliases.
7. If you sample at 5 kHz, the time interval between samples is 1/5 ms or 0.2 ms.
8. The audio signal at 1100 Hz should be sampled at a frequency of at least 2200 Hz to
be faithfully reproduced. This converts to a sampling period of 1/2.2 ms, 0.45 ms.
Therefore 0.5 ms is not often enough.
9. The alias frequency is given by fs - fo. The sampling frequency is 1/0.5 ms, which is 2.0
kHz. The signal frequency is 1.1 kHz. Therefore the alias frequency = 2.0 - 1.1 = 0.9 kHz.
10. For a signal at 25 Hz, the sampling frequency should be greater than 50 Hz. Therefore
there must be an alias frequency, given by fs - fo, which is 50 - 25 = 15 Hz.
11. The Nyquist frequency is 20 Hz. The signal is 5 Hz above the Nyquist frequency so
the alias frequency is 5 Hz below, which is therefore 15 Hz. The frequency diagram looks

Tolerances
On eBay, resistors are sold with tolerances of 1% or 5%. For a potential divider, with a 6v

C = Q/V

Where does this energy come from? Ultimately from the chemical energy of the battery.
However, work must be done to get the electrons on the plate of the capacitor. They carry
a negative charge and repel each other. Work is done against that electric force. With
more push from the battery, by doubling the voltage, twice as many electrons can be
stored on the plate. This leads to four times as much energy. The capacitor gives up that
electrical energy when it is discharged.

Factors affecting capacitance
Area
With a larger area of plate more charge can be stored. The capacitance is proportional to
the area, which means if we double the area, we double the capacitance of the capacitor.
This is used in a variable air capacitor, where one plate is xed and the other plate can be
turned. Therefore the area of overlap of the two plates will change, changing the
capacitance.
The plates are made from thin metal lm or plastic with a metal coating. The lm is rolled
to reduce space.
Distance between the plate

Doubling the distance between the plates halves the capacitance. This is because the
electric force of the negative plate is weaker as it is further from the positive plate. If we
want a high value of capacitance, the distance needs to be made very small. This is
possible in electrolytic capacitors where the plates are very close together.
The dielectric
The dielectric is the insulating layer between the metal plates. It affects the strength of the
electric eld between the plates. It becomes polarised by the electric eld, meaning that
one end becomes positive and the other negative. It can be solid or a paste, even a liquid,
gel, air or a vacuum.
Ceramic
Ceramic capacitors are usually used as a bypass capacitor and for high frequency
applications.
Mica
Mica capacitors have low value capacitance, are stable and reliable, and are used at high
frequencies.
Electrolytic
The electrolyte is usually paper soaked in an electrolyte. The capacitor is made from a thin
lm of aluminium. The dielectric is a layer of aluminium oxide formed when a voltage is
applied. They can have large values of capacitance.
Plastic
Polyester lm has a high dielectric constant, resulting in high values of capacitance. They
are found in DC applications such as coupling and decoupling. Polycarbonate capacitors
are very stable and are used in circuits with a xed frequency.
Putting all the factors together, the formula for the capacitance of a capacitor in terms of
the area of one of its plates A, the distance between the plates d, and the permittivity k is
given by

C = kA/d
A has the units m, distance in m and k in F/m. This is the formula given in the speci cation,
but I prefer the symbol Œµ for permittivity. Therefore the formula becomes

C = ŒµA/d
We have met the permittivity of free space Œµo before. The permittivity of the dielectric is
related to Œµo by the relative permittivity of the dielectric, Œµr which is just a number. For
example, the relative permittivity of mica is 5.

Œµ = Œµo x Œµ r
What can go wrong?
Apart from putting a polarised capacitor in the wrong way, what else can go wrong?
Exceeding the DC working voltage! If the voltage is too high there is the risk that the
dielectric breaks down. A short circuit could follow with a spark.
Dielectric loss is another problem, where the alternating electric eld is too fast for the
dielectric at high frequencies. The dielectric cannot switch fast enough and energy is lost
as heat.

Parallel resonance
In a parallel RLC resonant circuit, the impedance graph is the opposite to that of series
resonance with a peak impedance at the resonant frequency. Therefore the current is a
minimum at resonance. The voltages across the capacitor and inductor are equal and
opposite so they cancel, but again the voltages can be quite high. The capacitor and

series resonant circuit.

In summary,
Series resonance = low impedance
Parallel resonance = high impedance.

Q factor
At intermediate we saw the series resonance curve where the impedance is simply
resistive because the impedances of the capacitor and inductor cancel. The quality Q
factor of the resonance curve is a measure of its sharpness as we do not want a broad
response curve.
Q in terms of component values
One way of de ning Q for series resonance is as the ratio of the reactance of either the
capacitor or inductor to the resistance.

Q = X/R for series resonance
This is a good de nition because the inductance is related to the store of either magnetic
or electric energy while the resistance represents the wasted energy as heat. With more
resistance the Q factor becomes lower, which is not so good. As both X and R are
measured in ohms, Q is just a number.
(The formula for parallel resonance is the opposite, but it is not in the speci cation.)
For example, the resonance of a series RLC circuit occurs when the reactance of the
capacitor and inductor are equal at 1200 ‚Ñ¶. If the resistance in the circuit is 50 ‚Ñ¶, what is
the Q factor? Q = X/R = 1200/50 = 24.
If resistance is now 40 ‚Ñ¶, how does this change the Q factor? Q = X/R = 1200/40 = 30.
The formulae for the Q factor in terms of the component values of C and L are given in
the speci cation. For the sake of completeness, they are Q = 2œÄfL/R or 1/2œÄfCR.
It can be shown that the Q factor is also the ratio of the voltage of either the capacitor or
inductor, as they are equal, to the voltage of the supply. This is quite terrifying! The
voltages across the components and currents in the circuit can be so high that arcing and
melting of solder can occur! Components need to be rated for these high voltages,
particularly capacitors.

How the ampli er works
The transistor is biased at DC with steady values of current and voltage. Along comes the
oscillating input. When the positive half of the cycle is applied to the base, the base
current increases as does the collector current. The voltage across the load increases but
the voltage across the transistor decreases. The opposite happens on the negative half of
the signal.

Therefore the signal is ampli ed and reversed in phase to the input. The peak of the input
relates to the trough of the output.

Classes of bias
Depending on the bias or operating point, there are several classes of bias - class A,
class B, class AB and class C.
We can see the di erences in the transfer graphs for each of them.
Class A
In class A the transistor is biased at the mid-point of the linear part of the output
characteristics. This means the output will be faithful copy of the input. There will be no
distortion. This is the class we have been using so far with the ampli er.
The downside of class A is that current is still passing in the transistor with no input.
Therefore the e ciency is low. This bias is used for low power small signal audio
frequency ampli ers, such as car radios.

Modulation
This chapter is all about phase and how it can be used to send digital signals. Phase was

Phasor diagrams
We have seen the advantage of phasor diagrams in showing the phase relationships of

magnitude of a quantity such as the voltage across a resistor and its phase relative to the

However the rules of vector addition can be used for phasors.
We can apply the same principles of phasor diagrams to radio waves.
On a phasor diagram a radio wave would have an amplitude, a frequency and a phase
relative to a reference. For example, a radio wave of 6 Œºv and 50 MHz, can be shown at

I and Q waveforms
How do phasor diagrams help us to understand the di erent kinds of modulation, and
phase modulation in particular? We need to introduce the concept of I and Q waveforms.
A radio wave can be produced by combining two waves which are out of phase by 90
degrees. E ectively, we are combining a sine wave with a cosine wave. The green wave is
a sine wave and the blue is a cosine wave. The resultant is the red wave. We can see that
its peak is halfway between the two waves, so its phase is 45 degrees.

The phasor diagram shows the green phasor along the x axis and the blue phasor along
the y axis. The resultant is the red phasor. The x axis is called the in-phase or incident or I

axis, while the y axis is the 90 degrees out of phase, or the quadrature Q axis. As the
phasors rotate, they keep their phase relationships.

Digital modulation
There are many advantages of digital modulation over analogue modulation. For example,

We saw in the Intermediate manual the di erent kinds of digital modulation. Common

Oscillators
At Intermediate level you studied the transistor oscillator, the quartz crystal oscillator and
the variable frequency oscillator and had a quick look at the Direct Digital Synthesiser,
which generates audio and RF signals from pre-set digital values held in a memory, or
Lookup Table. Now we will study digital oscillators in more detail.
The Phase Locked Loop PLL
In a phase locked loop synthesiser, the output frequency of the voltage controlled
oscillator is continuously compared to a xed reference oscillator. If there is a phase
di erence between them, then a voltage is produced to bring them back in phase. If the
output frequency drops slightly, there will be a phase lag. This lag is detected by the
phase detector which will produce a voltage. This voltage is ltered before passing to the
voltage controlled oscillator VCO where it corrects the frequency. Therefore the phase of
the two frequencies are locked.
The frequency of the reference oscillator fref and the frequency of the VCO fo both have to
be divided by an appropriate number to make them equal. For example if the reference
oscillator frequency, fref, is 6.000 MHz, it is divided by A = 6000, to reduce it to 1 kHz.
Similarly if the VCO frequency, fo is 10.000 MHz, it must be divided by N = 10,000. If the
output frequency of the VCO is changed to 10.500 MHz, it must now be divided by
10,500.
Therefore fref /A = fo/N = 1 kHz.

Instability and thermal drift
The stability of an oscillator was discussed in the Intermediate manual. Here we look at
the thermal e ects.
Oscillators experience thermal drift where their frequency changes as the temperature
increases. This could cause you to operate outside a band if you are near the edge of a
band or to transmit over another operator‚Äôs transmission. How would you know that you
are drifting? Has another operator told you that you are drifting? Is their receiver drifting?!

Component values of capacitance and inductance can change with temperature causing
the resonant frequency to change. A transmitter warms up after a while to a steady
temperature as the ampli ers and other units generate waste heat. However, the oscillator
should be designed not to be a ected by changes in temperature as your transmit. It
should be located away from heat sources. Its components should have temperature
coe cients such that any change in the values for L and C cancel out.
Parasitic oscillations
Parasitic or spurious oscillations are unwanted oscillations which result in emissions at
unpredictable frequencies. They are called parasitic because they take power for their
own use. They can occur at frequencies unrelated to the desired frequency and cause
severe interference to other radio users. Parasitic oscillations are due to stray capacitance
or inductance in RF ampli er stages and tend to occur above the operating frequency.
Self oscillations may occur at or near the operating frequency. The cure is to minimise the
feedback by screening the input and output circuits.
Also oscillations may occur at lower frequencies than the operating frequency due to
instability in the audio stages. To prevent any oscillation, chance resonances need to be
damped by adding a resistor and a DC blocking capacitor.
Phase noise
An ideal oscillator will produce a single frequency. In practice the lack of stability will
cause the phase of the frequency to change. This can be shown on a frequency domain
diagram with the phase noise, extending to positive and negative values beside the
output frequency. This noise will carry through to all the mixing circuits in the transmitter.
The output will be distorted and spread in frequency.

To give a measure of the phase noise, we need to specify the o set from the carrier
frequency. This could be 1 kHz, 10 kHz or 100 kHz depending on the carrier frequency.

The function of the modulator is to add the information, in the form of voice or data, to the
carrier radio frequency wave. We can do this either by changing the amplitude of the

AM
An amplitude modulated signal has the carrier frequency and the two sidebands. The
upper sideband is the sum of the carrier frequency and the signal while the lower is the
di erence. For example, if the signal is 1 kHz and the carrier frequency is 6.0 MHz, the

SSB
The modulator for Single Sideband, SSB, requires just one sideband and no carrier
frequency. This is achieved with a balanced modulator which suppresses the carrier
frequency, leaving the two sidebands. It is now a double sideband suppressed carrier
signal. A narrow band lter cancels any remaining carrier frequency and the unwanted
sideband.
FM
A modulator to produce frequency modulation has a varactor diode, which is a reversed
bias diode whose capacitance varies with the applied voltage. The audio input signal is
applied to the varactor diode, which forms part of a resonant oscillator. The oscillating
audio signal changes the capacitance of the varactor which in turn changes the resonant
centre frequency of the oscillator. For example, a 1 kHz signal and a carrier of 7.075 MHz,
with a deviation of 2.5 kHz, would oscillate from 7.0725 MHz to 7.0775 MHz, a thousand
times a second. The amplitude of the 1 kHz signal determines the deviation.

Ampli ers
The job of the ampli er is to increase the signal su ciently to drive the antenna or dummy
load. The requirements are di erent for the di erent kinds of modulation. If the ampli er is
an early stage ampli er it is called a driver.
For an FM transmitter, the signal amplitude is constant. A class C ampli er can be used
because we are not worried about distortion in the ampli cation. Class C ampli ers are
non-linear but this is not an issue if the amplitude does not change.
However, we are concerned with linearity for AM and SSB ampli ers. The ampli ed signal
must be a faithful reproduction of the original signal, allowing no distortion. Therefore we
nd class B linear ampli ers in AM and SSB transmitters.

RF power ampli er
The RF power ampli er must provide linear ampli cation for AM and SSB modes because
they require the modulated signal to match the audio signal. There must be no distortion.
This means the ampli er is a class A ampli er. It must not be over-driven, as this could
produce harmonics which would be outside the signal bandwidth. This is called splatter.

What can go wrong?
Over-modulating
Over-modulating a transmitter distorts the modulated audio signal as shown in the
Intermediate manual. This will cause harmonics of the audio. The audio is limited to 3
kHz, so they will be multiples of 2 or 3, giving 6 to 9 kHz, making the transmitted
bandwidth excessive.
With FM the equivalent problem is over-deviation, resulting in extra FM sidebands.
Similarly, there will be interference to adjacent channels.
Over-drive
Over-drive of the RF power ampli er will cause it to become non-linear. This will produce
harmonics, e ectively increasing the transmitted bandwidth. These harmonics will cause
intermodulation products.

Receivers
To build a radio receiver you need oscillators, multipliers, mixers, demodulators and

Speci cations
Sensitivity
The sensitivity of a receiver measures its ability to discern the weakest signal. For
example, the sensitivity of the Kenwood TS-890s receiver (https://www.kenwood.com/
usa/com/amateur/ts-890s/ ) is quoted for SSB as 0.2 ŒºV for the range 1.705 to 24.5 MHz,
for a signal to noise ratio of 10 dB. This means that a signal of 0.2 ŒºV produces a
discernible signal above the noise level, which is ten times the power of the noise.
For FM, the sensitivity is quoted as 0.22 ŒºV for the range 28 to 30 MHz for 12 dB SINAD.
The ratio SINAD refers to the ratio of the signal to the noise and distortion, where any
distortion of the signal is added to the noise value. 12 dB means that the signal will be 16
times greater. (12 = 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 which converts to 2 x 2 x 2 x 2).
The weakest discernible signal can also be expressed in dBm, which is decibels with
respect to one milliwatt. 0 dBm is therefore 1 mW. +10 dBm is the same as 10 mW.
If the input impedance is 50 ‚Ñ¶, the power can be calculated from P= V x V/R. For a signal
of 0.5 ŒºV, this converts to -113 dBm.
The signal to noise ratio, SNR, is de ned as
SNR = 10 log (power of signal/power of noise)
If the SNR = 10, that means log (power of signal/power of noise) = 1
In which case, the power of the signal = 10 x power of the noise.
An SNR of 10 dB is taken as the worst acceptable value. In audio engineering an SNR of
60 dB is considered good.
Because it is not always convenient to measure SNR directly, the related measurement
signal plus noise-to-noise ratio ([S+N]/N) is used. Mathematically, (S+N)/N = S/N+1. While
the relationship is simple when expressed as simple ratios, in dB, the di erence between
the two measurements depends on their value. For example, a ([S+N]/N) ratio of 3 dB
equates to an SNR of 0 dB. At the more usual level of 10 dB ([S+N]/N), the SNR is 9.54
dB. Another related measure that is increasingly popular is signal-plus-noise-plusdistortion to noise-plus-distortion ratio (SINAD). This measurement is essentially the same
as ([S+N]/N) with distortion included in the noise term. This is useful because, for most
users, the distortion is no more usable than the noise.

Image frequency
If only it were that simple! There are a few complications.
There is another signal frequency that can produce an intermediate frequency of 500 kHz.

If the local oscillator is 1500 kHz, what frequency can mix with it to produce 500 kHz?
A signal at 2.0 MHz will too!
2000 - 1500 = 500 Hz.
The image frequency is twice the intermediate frequency higher than the local oscillator.

This image frequency must be ltered before it meets the mixer.

There‚Äôs another problem!

Beat Frequency Oscillator
Morse code signals are simply a carrier wave, no carrier, a longer carrier wave, no carrier,

The answer is beats again. Inside the superheterodyne receiver is a beat frequency

To recover single sideband audio, rather than use a diode to demodulate, we use a

oscillator, the Beat Frequency Oscillator. It produces a single frequency, rather than a
variable frequency, as for direct conversion. The sum and di erence frequency signals are
generated. The result is the original modulating signal. When mixing the frequencies, we
are e ectively multiplying them, so the word ‚Äòproduct‚Äô is applicable.

FM discriminator
In frequency modulation, the audio signal changes the frequency of the signal. The FM
discriminator is a detector which produces an output voltage that depends on the input
signal frequency. At the centre frequency the output is zero. If the frequency goes below
that value, the output is positive. If the frequency goes above, the output is negative. As
there is a linear relationship between the deviation of the frequency and the output
voltage, we get a faithful reproduction of the original audio signal.

Baluns
The dipole antenna is a balanced antenna and should be fed with balanced 50‚Ñ¶ twin

Transformer balun
A transformer balun consists of two separate windings usually on a ferrite core. The core
must be able to cope with the frequencies and power levels. The unbalanced coax is
connected to ground via one of the windings. The other winding has its centre grounded,
providing the balanced outputs above and below the ground point.
The transformer balun can also be used to match impedances by changing the ratio of
the number of turns for the primary Np and the secondary Ns.

primary and secondary. A 4:1 balun has twice the number of turns on the secondary
because the impedance is proportional to the square of the turns ratio.

A choke balun has several ferrite rings on the feeder. This prevents signals from travelling

Antenna Matching Units
If all is well and the impedances of the transmitter, feed line and antenna are all the same
at 50 ‚Ñ¶, the system is matched. This means all the power is transmitted, none is
re ected. It there is a mis-match, power will be re ected and the SWR readings could be
greater than 2:1. Not good!
To improve matters an Antenna Matching Unit, AMU, is inserted between the transmitter
and feed line. Ideally the AMU should be placed before the antenna but this is not always
practical.

The AMU is adjusted so that the impedance of the transmitter matches that of the feed
line. Placing the AMU at the end of the feed line, ensures that the feed line is matched to
the antenna. Inside the AMU are variable capacitors and inductors which cancel any
capacitive or inductive reactance in the feeder input. This leaves a resistive value, usually
50 ‚Ñ¶. How do you know when this has been achieved? The SWR reading on the AMU will
be the indicator. However creating a match between the transmitter and feed line does
not guarantee a match between the feed line and antenna. For example, if the SWR in the
feed line is 4:1, it will still be 4:1 even when the AMU presents the load to the transmitter
as 50 ‚Ñ¶.
You need to be able to identify the three main circuits for an AMU. - T, œÄ and an inverted
L. They are shown in the diagrams on page 142 and can be recognised by their shapes.

The propagation of radio waves was discussed in the chapter on radio waves, in terms of
the oscillations of the electric and magnetic elds. Here we consider the ionosphere
where harmful UV is absorbed by the Earth‚Äôs atmosphere, separating electrons from
atoms. HF propagation is only possible because of the Sun‚Äôs radiation. We consider the

Also in this section we look at the indicators for good propagation that are available for

The Inverse Square Law
When you drop a stone in a pond, the ripples get smaller as the wave travels outwards.
The energy is spread over a wider and wider circle. We know the energy is getting weaker
as the wave goes out further because the amplitude of the wave is less. In the same way
the energy from an antenna or a light source gets weaker as the wave travels but this is
now in three dimensions. The electromagnetic energy from a source of waves spreads out
as a sphere. A faint star is further away than a bright star. The energy falling on a surface
will decrease as the distance from the source increases.
The energy of a wave depends on the square of the amplitude. For a sound wave the
amplitude is displacement of the air molecules. For a radio wave the amplitude is the
electric eld strength, so the energy depends on the square of the electric eld strength.
We saw in the chapter on Radio Waves that the power ux density is measured in W/m2.
The inverse square law tells us that if we double the distance, the power ux density is
reduced by a quarter. Treble the distance, it is nine times weaker. If we plot the power ux
density against distance, we can see the rapid change.

Graph of energy ux against distance.
The Inverse Square Law is applicable to all kinds of waves. The relevance of this law to
amateur radio is well done if your antenna can receive signals from the other side of the
planet. QRP operating is your battle against the inverse square law!

Sunspot number
In normal conditions a state of dynamic equilibrium exists, with the Sun causing ionisation
while the electrons recombine with the ions. The level of radiation from the Sun depends
on the number of sunspots, which are the slightly cooler regions of the Sun‚Äôs surface. The
sunspot number is the number of sunspots and sunspot groups on the Sun‚Äôs surface
facing the Earth. For example, the website spaceweather.com tells me that the number is
151. In 2019 there were 281 days with no sunspots at all, so 151 is signi cant. As the
number increases so does the amount of UV, causing more ionisation in the ionosphere.
This is good news as it improves propagation on the bands above 10 MHz, even at night,
raising the maximum useable frequency, MUF. However the lowest useable frequency,
LUF, is also increased, with the low frequency bands of 80 and 160 metres su ering from
more absorption. When the cycle is at a minimum, the reverse is true, with good
propagation for 80 and 160 metres, but not so good for 20 MHz.

The Solar Flux Index, SFI, is another indicator of solar activity. It measures the amount of

MUF will be low. A high value means that there is enough ionisation to permit DX-ing at

Solar ares
Occasionally, a solar are erupts from the Sun, releasing large amounts of energy with X
rays as well as UV. Travelling at the speed of light it reaches the Earth‚Äôs atmosphere 8
minutes later, increasing the ionisation. The are also disrupts the Earth‚Äôs protective
geomagnetic eld. A big solar are, called an X-class are, can cause radio blackout,
called a sudden ionospheric disturbance, SID. Sudden because it happens so quickly,
only 8 minutes from leaving the Sun. There will be a high degree of ionisation in the D
layer resulting in attenuation of radio frequencies. For the higher layers of the ionosphere
this can improve conditions for the higher frequency bands before black out occurs.
The measure of geomagnetic activity is the Kp index. Today it is 0.67, quiet. A Kp value of
4 would indicate a degradation of HF propagation while a value of 6 indicates a large
storm. A Kp value of 9 would mean a very major storm which would disrupt HF
communications as well as satellite communications.
So a solar ux of 200 sfu and a Kp of 4 show that the space weather is unsettled.

As well as emitting radiation, the Sun also emits charged particles, called the solar wind.
They interact with the Earth‚Äôs geomagnetic eld and can cause a geomagnetic storm. A
coronal mass ejection, CME, sends out a vast number of energetic particles. They can
take about 20 to 40 hours to reach Earth and we can sometimes see the e ect with the
Northern Lights, Aurora Borealis, visible at low latitudes.

Maximum Useable Frequency MUF
The Maximum Useable Frequency will be 3 to 4 times the critical frequency. It depends
on several factors:
- The time of day, with a peak between 10.00 and 16.00
- The sunspot number. It is much higher at the sunspot maximum than the minimum due
to the increased ionisation
- The season. It is much higher in the winter than the summer.
- The path. It depends on the skip distance.

How do you communicate with a station beyond the range of a ground wave, but in the
skip zone? You could attempt using NVIS. Frequencies below the critical frequency will
return to Earth, but higher frequency waves go o into space. Therefore you transmit at a

Cross Polarisation
Polarisation was discussed in the chapter on radio waves. At HF the ionosphere can
change the direction of polarisation of the wave. Therefore the orientation of your antenna
is not an issue at HF. You can use a vertical or a horizontal antenna, it doesn‚Äôt matter.
However, at VHF and UHF it does matter. Your receiving antenna should have the same
orientation as the polarisation of the transmitted signal. If you try to receive a horizontally
polarised wave with a vertically polarised antenna, the signal strength will be reduced as
much as 20 dB. This known as cross polarisation.

Earth Moon bounce
For Earth-Moon-Earth propagation, or moon bounce, all you need to do is to point your
antenna at the Moon so that your signal will bounce o , to be received elsewhere on
Earth. If only it were that easy!
There are several requirements as it is a huge challenge.
Both stations need to be able to see the Moon, ideally at its perigee when the Moon is
closest to the Earth and we get a Super moon. The distance is about 363,000 km.
The transmitter must be very powerful, as the Moon is so far away and its surface is a
poor re ector. It is hardly a smooth surface with so many craters, so only about 6% gets
re ected. Obviously a directional antenna with high gain is needed. Also it needs to be
steerable to track the Moon‚Äôs orbit.

dB on 1.296 GHz. You can see his set up in the photo, which is quite modest compared

Auroral propagation
An aurora, also know as the Northern Lights or Southern Lights (Aurora Borealis or Aurora
Australis) occurs after a coronal mass ejection. The solar wind produces a lot of ionisation

and a colourful light show at high north and south latitudes, as the charged particles
collide with oxygen and nitrogen molecules in the E layer.
For the radio amateur, the aurora creates an auroral curtain of ions which can re ect
signals on the VHF bands.
The signals should be directed north towards the aurora so that they bounce o the
curtain. As the curtain is constantly changing and is not static, the movement causes the
signal to be distorted and utter rapidly. CW and digital modes are the best to use but the
CW signals have a buzzing sound.

Hilderstone Radio Society member Andrew G0MBL told me he gave auroral propagation
a try on 2 metres and he was able to work Scotland, Norway, Sweden and Finland, using
10 W on SSB and a small 6 element ZL Yagi. He said the sound of the signal is very hard
to describe, but once heard it is never forgotten!

Galactic Noise

Our radio signals travel out into space. Space sends radio waves to Earth. The
waves have a range of frequencies and contribute to a random noise level which
can be heard in a radio receiver. Their origin is electrical activity in the cosmos. The
galactic, or cosmic noise, increases when the antenna is directed at the Sun,
Jupiter or the Milky Way.

Link Budget
When designing a communication link between two sites, there are many factors to
consider. Link budget analysis takes into account all the gains and losses in the link. The
propagation path length and its loss, the transmitter power, feeder losses and antenna
gain are all considered. Knowing the transmitted power the strength of the received signal
can be calculated. The path loss includes the weakening of the signal as it travels or
spreads, as well as any losses due to obstructions such as buildings and hills.
There is an app that can calculate the link budget available here (https://
www.mathworks.com/help/comm/ug/link-budget-analysis.html) and a formula in this
website (https://www.electronics-notes.com/articles/antennas-propagation/propagationoverview/radio-link-budget-formula-calculator.php)
The calculation can be done using dB. For example, a transmitter produces a power of 20
dB into an antenna of gain 6 dB, However there is a feeder loss of 2 dB and a path loss of
- 50 dB. At the receiver, the feeder loss is also 2 dB but the antenna gain is 10 dB. What
is the received power in dB?
Answer = 20 + 6 - 2 - 50 - 2 +10 = -18 dB
What if the power of the transmitter is given in watts and not dB? For example, 10 W. You
have to convert the watts in dBm, which for 10 W is 40 dBm. The link budget analysis will
give a result in dBm, which you can compare with the sensitivity of the receiver. For the
Kenwood TS-890s it is 0.2 ŒºV, which is -121 dBm.
There are more examples of path loss in the chapter on measurements and decibels.

Safety
How safe is your radio equipment? You may nd that your are asked to help your club
with a special event station, so you need to be aware of the risks involved. With the
licence allowing you to transmit with 1000 W of power, safety has become paramount.
These are some precautions you need to take in your shack.

High Voltages
Voltages as low as 30 volts can be dangerous. For British Gas electricians, the maximum
touch voltage is 50 volts. Involuntary muscle spasms can be caused by a few
milliamperes. 100 mA can stop your heart. One electrician‚Äôs advice is ‚Äòbe vigilant‚Äô.

Electrical Precautions
Some equipment may use valves which operate at voltage over 200 volts, with some
ampli ers using over 2 kV.
High value capacitors can hold their charge for a long time, as long as days!
All equipment should have on e master switch, its position known to your family or club
members. The switch controlling mains supplies should be double pole, to isolate the live
and neutral.
Indicator lamps show that equipment is live. If faulty they must be replaced immediately.
Switch o equipment before inspecting.
If you need to have the equipment on and working, use only hand with the other in your
pocket. This prevents current going from arm to arm across your heart. Ideally have
another person working with you who knows CPR. Wear shoes with an insulated sole.
Remove watches and jewellery.
Test probes should be good quality insulated tools
Don‚Äôt wear headphones in case a sudden noise startles you.

When operating in temporary premises or outdoors further hazards are introduced.

Consider trailing cables as a trip hazard.
Use correct fuses and check for damage and to leads
Use RCBO‚Äôs.
If the ground is damp, use a rubber mat
Do not make adjustments or repairs to live equipment.
If you are operating abroad be aware that mains supplies may be a di erent voltage or
frequency.
Also di erent plugs and sockets may not be suitable for UK equipment.
Check RF exposure to the public.
Cables must be waterproof for outdoor use.
For indoors consider the routing of cables and the need to cover with a rubber protector.
Do a written risk assessment to identify any hazards and what measures are needed to

Lightning
There is already a lot of electricity in the atmosphere, never mind in the ionosphere, even
close to the ground. On an ordinary day, over at ground or over the sea, the electric
potential increases by about 100 volts for every metre upwards. (https://
www.feynmanlectures.caltech.edu/II_09.html) There is a potential di erence of about 200
volts between your nose and your feet!
According to NASA (https://lightning.nsstc.nasa.gov/primer/primer2.html ) ice particles in
a cloud grow, collide and break apart. The smaller particles acquire a positive voltage,
while the larger particles acquire a negative charge. Gravity and updraft cause the
particles to separate, producing a charge separation of enormous potential within the
cloud and between the cloud and ground. The voltage in thunderstorms can reach
millions of volts. Eventually the electric potential overcomes the resistance of the air and
lightning strikes in the form of an electrical discharge. A typical lightning strike, or stroke,
can consist of many discharges, as many as ten. Each stroke can have a current from 2
to 200 thousand amperes. You can imagine the damage that would cause to your radio!

The E ect of the Test Meter
An analogue milliammeter uses a moving coil inside a magnetic eld to detect a current. It

Frequency measurements
Crystal calibrators
It is essential that you do not transmit outside the bands. Therefore you need an accurate
means of measuring frequency using a frequency counter.
The basic kind is a crystal calibrator, which has a 10 MHz crystal oscillator. Logic chips
provide frequency calibration markers at 1 MHz, 100 kHz and 10 kHz.
Digital frequency counters
The digital frequency counter uses a crystal controlled 1 MHz reference oscillator.
Obviously, it needs to be accurate and precise so temperature stability is important. A
gate allows the signal through and counts the number of cycles in a given time, such as
one millisecond, and the result is passed to the display. The duration of the gate opening
is crucial to give an accurate measurement. If the duration is too short some cycles may
be missed. A one millisecond gate is only accurate to 1 kHz while a one second gate is
accurate to 1 Hz. For example, for a frequency of 135.8 kHz and a gate time of 1 ms, only
135 cycles will be counted.

Decibels
Using combinations of 3 and 10 dB
A power gain of 3 dB is the same as doubling the power and 10 dB is a power increase of
10 times. If there is a minus sign, it means a power loss or attenuation.
For example, the input signal to an ampli er is 1 mW and the output is 10 W, what is the
power gain in dB? The power gain is 10W/0.001W = 10,000 times. We know from above,
that a 10 dB is the same as a power increase of 10 times. Because 10,000 = 10 x 10 x 10
x 10, we change the x sign to a plus +, so we have 10 + 10 + 10 + 10 = 40 dB. The power
gain in decibels is 40 dB.
To change dB back into a power gain, you have to change the pluses into times, and
minuses into divides. You can see that with the 40 dB, we just reverse the procedure.
40 dB = 10 dB + 10 dB + 10 dB + 10 dB.
But 10 dB is a gain of 10 times, so we change the pluses into times,
Power gain = 10 x 10 x 10 x 10 = 10,000 times.
Now we play Countdown! Using just the numbers 3 and 10, how can you make 14?
Answer is + 10 + (10 - 3 - 3).
So what is 14 dB expressed as a power gain, which is speci cally mentioned in the
speci cation, page 2.30? Doing the brackets rst. (10 - 3 - 3) converts to a gain of 10,
followed by a loss of half, followed by another half, which is 10/2 x 1/2 = 10/4 = 2.5.
So the expression + 10 + (10 - 3 - 3) is the same as a gain of 10 times and a gain of 2.5.
Therefore, the whole gain becomes 10 x 2.5, which is 25 times. 14 dB is a gain of 25 W.

gain or loss = 20 log (Vout/Vin)

gain = 20 log (1 V/1 mV) = 20 log 1000 = 20 x 3 = 60 dBV.
